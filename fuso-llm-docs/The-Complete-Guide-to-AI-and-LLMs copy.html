Chapter 01

The Road to Transformers

Large Language Models didn't spring from nowhere. They descend from a specific lineage of ideas in sequence modeling — the problem of teaching machines to process ordered data like sentences, where each word's meaning depends on the words around it.

Recurrent Neural Networks (1990s–2010s)

The first serious approach: RNNs process a sentence one word at a time, maintaining a hidden "state" that carries forward context. The fatal flaw: the vanishing gradient problem. Information from early words faded over long sequences — like a game of telephone degrading with each step.

LSTMs — A Partial Fix (1997)

Long Short-Term Memory networks added "gates" to selectively remember or forget information — like a notebook the model carries. They powered Google Translate for years, but were still sequential: each word had to wait for the previous one. Slow and impossible to parallelize.

RNNs process sequentially (slow). Transformers process all tokens at once using attention (fast, parallelizable).

The Attention Breakthrough (2014)

Bahdanau et al. introduced attention — letting the model dynamically focus on relevant parts of the input at each step, instead of compressing everything into one state vector. Like giving the model a spotlight it could point at any part of the input.

"Attention Is All You Need" (2017)

Google researchers threw away the recurrent part entirely and used only attention. The result: the Transformer. Every major LLM today — GPT, Claude, Gemini, LLaMA — is a Transformer.

Chapter 02

Tokenization & Embeddings

Before an LLM processes language, it converts text into numbers. Two steps.

Tokenization: Text → Pieces

A tokenizer splits input into tokens — roughly word-sized but not always clean boundaries. Common words are single tokens. Rarer words get split: "understanding" → "under" + "standing."

Text is split into sub-word tokens, each mapped to a numeric ID. Each ID maps to a learnable embedding vector.

This matters because LLMs have a context window — max tokens it can see at once. Claude: 200K tokens (~500 pages). GPT-4o: ~128K. Everything — your prompt, documents, the model's response — must fit.

Embeddings: Words as Points in Space

Each token becomes a vector of hundreds of numbers representing its meaning. Similar words cluster together geometrically.

Real embeddings have 768–4096 dimensions. Semantic similarity = geometric proximity.

Crucially, these embeddings aren't fixed. As tokens pass through Transformer layers, attention enriches them with context. "Bank" in "river bank" becomes very different from "bank" in "bank account."

Chapter 03

The Attention Mechanism

When you read "The animal didn't cross the street because it was too tired," you know "it" = "animal." Self-attention gives LLMs this same ability.

Query, Key, Value

Each token generates three vectors: Query (what am I looking for?), Key (what do I contain?), Value (what information do I provide?). The model matches Queries against Keys to find relevant tokens, then reads their Values. Like a library: Query = your search, Key = book labels, Value = book contents.

Attention scores show how much each word matters when processing "it." The model heavily attends to "animal" — resolving the reference.

Multi-Head Attention

The model runs 12–96 attention computations in parallel, each learning different relationship types: one tracks grammar, another tracks pronoun references, another captures topic. Results are concatenated, giving the model simultaneous awareness of syntax, semantics, and more.

Chapter 04

The Transformer Architecture

Here's how the pieces combine into the full system powering every major LLM.

Input → tokenize → embed → pass through dozens of attention+FFN layers → predict next token. Then loop: feed "Paris" back in and predict the next token.

Key details: Positional encoding tells the model word order. Residual connections (skip connections) prevent information loss across dozens of layers. Feed-forward networks store factual knowledge in their weights. The model generates one token at a time, autoregressively.

Why This Matters to You

Each new token is shaped by everything before it in the context window. A well-structured prompt isn't just aesthetics — it directly influences what the model generates next.

Chapter 05

How LLMs Are Trained

Pre-training builds knowledge by next-token prediction across the internet. SFT teaches the assistant format. RLHF aligns behavior with human preferences. RLVR (2024–25's breakthrough) trains against objectively verifiable problems, producing reasoning models that genuinely think step-by-step.

Temperature controls randomness in output. Low (0–0.3) = deterministic, focused. High (0.7–1.0+) = creative, varied. Use low for factual work, higher for creative tasks.

Chapter 07

Prompt Engineering

This is probably the most practically useful skill for working with LLMs day to day.

1
Be Specific

"Write a 200-word B2B email announcing our Gantt chart feature to engineering managers, professional tone, clear CTA for free trial" beats "write me a marketing email."

2
Provide Context

The model only knows what you tell it + training. Paste documents, explain situations, define the audience.

3
Define Output Format

Bullet list? Table? JSON? 3-paragraph memo? Specify it or the model guesses.

4
Assign a Role

"You are a senior financial analyst" activates relevant patterns. Keep roles realistic and task-relevant.

5
Iterate

Prompting is a conversation. "Close but more concise" or "tone is too formal" gives the model signal. Best results: 2–4 rounds.

Anatomy of a Great Prompt

Role: Who should the AI act as? Context: What background does it need? Task: What exactly should it do? Format: How should the output be structured? Constraints: Length limits, what to avoid? Examples: (Optional) What does good output look like?
❌ Weak
Analyze our sales data.
✅ Strong
Role: Senior data analyst at a retail company.

Context: Q4 online revenue dropped 15% vs Q3 but in-store rose 8%. Marketing flat. Loyalty program launched Oct.

Task: Analyze causes. 3 hypotheses ranked by likelihood, each with data to examine.

Format: 3-sentence exec summary, then hypothesis sections.
Constraints: Under 500 words. Specific to our situation.

Key principle: tell what to do, not what to avoid. "Write concisely in active voice" beats "Don't be wordy, don't use passive voice."

Chapter 08

Advanced Techniques

Zero-Shot → Few-Shot → Chain-of-Thought

Zero-shot: Just ask, no examples. Try first — modern models often infer well.

Few-shot: 2–5 input/output examples before the real task. Highest-return technique — examples communicate patterns more precisely than descriptions.

Chain-of-thought: "Think through this step by step." Significantly improves performance on math, logic, and multi-step reasoning.

✅ Few-Shot Example
Classify by urgency (HIGH / MED / LOW):

"Charged twice" → HIGH
"Change password?" → LOW
"App crashes on open" → HIGH
"Last month's invoice copy" → MED

Classify: "Can't log in, presenting in 30 min"

Prompt Chaining

Break complex tasks into a sequence: extract → analyze → recommend. More reliable than one massive prompt.

Self-Reflection

"Produce the best answer. Then critique it — check assumptions, logic, edges. If issues found, revise." The generate-evaluate-revise loop catches errors.

Structured Output

Return as JSON: { "company": "", "revenue": "", "growth_rate": "", "key_risks": ["", ""] }
These Compound

Role + context + few-shot + chain-of-thought + format spec produces noticeably better results than a bare question. Each layer narrows the space of possible outputs toward what you need.

Chapter 09

Context Engineering

The LLM is the CPU, and its context window is the RAM. Context engineering is the delicate art and science of filling the context window with just the right information for the next step.
— Andrej Karpathy

The 2025 industry shift: from "prompt engineering" (what you ask) to context engineering (everything the model sees). Most failures aren't model incapability — they're missing context.

Four Strategies

W
Write — Save for later

Persist info externally (files, notes) for future retrieval.

S
Select — Pull in what's relevant

Retrieve only what matters (RAG). Don't stuff the window with everything.

C
Compress — Summarize

Distill long content to essentials. 50-page report → key findings.

I
Isolate — Separate concerns

Split across multiple focused calls. Specialized sub-agents > one overloaded agent.

Goldilocks

Too little context → model lacks what it needs. Too much → confusion, cost, degraded performance. Sweet spot for instructions: ~150–300 words.

Chapter 10

Hallucinations & Verification

LLMs are confident but not always correct. Verification is your responsibility.

Hallucinations are inherent to how these models work: they predict statistically likely text, not verified truth. Training rewards confidence over "I don't know." Common types: fabricated citations, incorrect facts stated confidently, logical drift in long outputs.

Other limits: Knowledge cutoff (can't know post-training events without search). Math errors (use code for critical calcs). Sycophancy (tends to agree — invite disagreement). Context drift (summarize key points in long chats). Inconsistency (same prompt ≠ same output).

Chapter 11

Using LLMs at Work

Writing & Communication

Email drafting: Context + tone + audience → draft. Ask for 2–3 approaches for difficult emails.

Editing: Paste your draft + specific request ("tighten prose," "simplify for non-technical"). AI is often better at editing than writing from scratch.

Meeting summaries: Transcript/notes → action items, decisions, open questions.

Analysis & Research

Document analysis: "What does section 4.2 commit us to?" "What are the three biggest risks?"

Devil's advocate: "What are the strongest arguments against this? Where could this fail?" Worth doing more often than most people think to.

Framework application: SWOT, Porter's Five Forces, pre-mortem — the model knows these deeply.

Code & Technical

Generation: Plain language → code. Always review and test.

Debugging: Error + code + full traceback. Remarkably good at diagnosis.

Explanation: "Explain line by line." Invaluable for onboarding to new codebases.

The 80/20

For most knowledge workers, 80% of AI value comes from: drafting/editing content, analyzing documents/data, brainstorming/strategic thinking, and writing/understanding code.

Workflow Checklists

Before Prompting

1. What exactly do I need?
2. What context does the model lack?
3. What output format?
4. Quality bar — draft or final?
5. How will I verify?

After Output

1. Does this match my need?
2. Key facts verifiable?
3. Reasoning sound?
4. What's missing?
5. What feedback for next iteration?

AI doesn't replace thinking — it amplifies it. The people who get the most out of these tools tend to be the ones who already think clearly about what they need.
