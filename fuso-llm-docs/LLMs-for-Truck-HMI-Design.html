Chapter 01

What Is an LLM?

A Large Language Model (LLM) is a type of AI trained on enormous amounts of text — books, websites, code, technical manuals — to learn the patterns of human language. Given some input text, it predicts what should come next. Scale this up to billions of internal parameters and trillions of training words, and the simple act of next-word prediction becomes surprisingly capable: it can write prose, generate code, analyze documents, and reason through multi-step problems.

For your work designing graphic display panels for FUSO trucks, this means you can use an LLM to draft HMI layout specifications, generate UI copy for cluster warning states, analyze ISO 15005 glance-time requirements against your designs, brainstorm icon sets for eCanter battery-level indicators, or review a CAN signal mapping document and extract the data points your instrument cluster needs to display.

LLMs don't "understand" in the way a human engineer does. They are pattern-matching systems that produce statistically likely text. The output can be remarkably useful — but it requires verification, especially for anything safety-critical like ASIL B telltale specifications.

Key Limitation

An LLM has no access to your internal FUSO design system, your Figma files, or your company's proprietary CAN database — unless you provide that information in your prompt. Think of it as a well-read colleague who has never worked at your company. The more context you give, the more useful it becomes.

Chapter 02

How We Got Here

LLMs didn't appear overnight. They are the result of decades of research in teaching machines to process sequences — like sentences, where each word's meaning depends on its neighbors.

1990s–2010s
Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs)
The first approach: process text one word at a time, carrying forward a "state." Worked for short sequences but forgot earlier words in long ones — the vanishing gradient problem.
1997
LSTMs
Added memory gates to selectively remember or forget. Powered early Google Translate. Still sequential — each word waited for the last — so training was slow.
2014
Attention Mechanisms
Let the model look back at all previous words and focus on the relevant ones. Like a spotlight the model could point at any part of the input.
2017
The Transformer ("Attention Is All You Need")
Google researchers removed the sequential processing entirely — using only attention. All words processed in parallel. This is the architecture behind every major LLM today.
2018–2020
GPT, BERT, GPT-3
Transformer-based language models trained on increasingly large datasets. GPT-3's 175 billion parameters showed emergent abilities — it could perform tasks from just a few examples in the prompt.
Nov 2022
ChatGPT
GPT-3.5 refined for conversation. 100 million users in two months. Made LLMs accessible to non-technical users.
2023–2024
Multimodal & Reasoning Models
GPT-4 added image understanding. Claude introduced 200K-token contexts (hundreds of pages). Reasoning models (o1) learned to think step-by-step, scoring 83% on math olympiad problems vs 13% for standard models.
2025–2026
Agents, FUSO-Hino Merger, ARCHION
AI shifted from chatting to acting — coding agents, research agents. In the truck industry, Daimler Truck concluded agreements integrating Mitsubishi Fuso and Hino Motors into ARCHION, with shared digital platforms and SDV (Software-Defined Vehicle) architectures becoming central.
Chapter 03

How LLMs Work

Step 1: Tokenization

The model breaks your text into tokens — sub-word pieces. "instrument cluster" might become ["instrument", " cluster"]. "eCanter" might become ["e", "C", "anter"]. Each token gets a numeric ID.

Note "telltale" gets split into two tokens. Rare or compound words are broken into sub-word pieces.

This matters because LLMs have a context window — a maximum number of tokens they can process at once. Claude handles ~200K tokens (roughly 500 pages). Everything you send — your prompt, any pasted spec documents, and the model's response — must fit inside this window.

Step 2: Embeddings

Each token becomes a vector — a list of hundreds of numbers representing its meaning in mathematical space. "Speedometer" and "tachometer" would be close together; "speedometer" and "refrigerator" would be far apart. As tokens pass through the model's layers, attention enriches these vectors with context — so "charge" in "battery charge level" becomes distinct from "charge" in "service charge."

Step 3: Prediction

After processing through dozens of Transformer layers (each applying attention + a feed-forward network), the model produces a probability distribution over its vocabulary for the next token. It picks one, outputs it, feeds the whole sequence back in, and predicts the next. This is why output streams in word-by-word.

What This Means for You

Each token the model generates is shaped by everything that came before it in the context window. The structure and content of your prompt directly influences every word of the output. A well-crafted prompt with specific HMI context produces fundamentally different results than a vague one.

Chapter 04

The Attention Mechanism

This is what makes Transformers work. For each token, the model computes how much it should "focus on" every other token to understand context.

When processing a sentence like "The ADAS telltale turned red because it detected an obstacle," attention lets the model figure out that "it" refers to the ADAS system, not the telltale or the obstacle. It does this by matching Queries ("what am I looking for?") against Keys ("what do I contain?") to produce attention scores, then using those scores to gather information from Values ("what do I provide?").

Chapter 06

Models Available Today

Claude (Anthropic)

200K token context — can process an entire vehicle spec document in one pass. Strong at analysis, writing, and code. Claude Code for agentic coding tasks.

GPT-5 / o3 (OpenAI)

Multimodal (text, image, audio). o3 reasoning model for complex logic. Strong at inferring intent from minimal prompts. Massive ecosystem.

Gemini 2.5 (Google)

Up to 2M token context. Deep Google Workspace integration. Prefers short, direct prompts with examples.

Open Source (DeepSeek R1, Qwen 3, LLaMA 4)

Can be run on local hardware for data-sensitive work — important if your CAN database or ECU architecture is proprietary and cannot leave company infrastructure.

For day-to-day HMI design work, any of the major models will serve you well. If you're working with large spec documents (ISO standards, vehicle architecture PDFs), Claude's large context window is especially useful. If you need image analysis of competitor dashboards, GPT-5 or Gemini's multimodal input is the better choice.

Chapter 07

Prompting for HMI Design Work

This chapter covers how to structure prompts specifically for the kind of work you do — designing graphic display panels for heavy-duty trucks. The principles apply regardless of which LLM you use.

The Five Principles

1
Be Specific

Don't say "design a dashboard." Say "I'm designing a 10.25-inch LCD instrument cluster for the FUSO Super Great 2025 facelift. The cluster needs to display SHIFTPILOT gear position, DPF regeneration status, and AdBlue level. What layout hierarchy would you recommend given ISO 15005's 1.5-second glance-time constraint?"

2
Provide Context

Paste your CAN signal list, your design system constraints, or the relevant section of the ISO standard. The model has no access to your Figma files or internal FUSO specs — you need to bring the context to it.

3
Define the Output

"Give me a table," "respond in JSON," "structure this as a Figma component spec with layer names." If you don't specify format, the model guesses.

4
Assign a Role

"You are a senior automotive HMI designer with experience in heavy-duty truck instrument clusters, familiar with ISO 26262 ASIL B requirements and Japanese commercial vehicle regulations."

5
Iterate

First output rarely perfect. "That's close, but the DPF status needs to be more prominent — it's a safety-critical telltale" gives the model signal to refine.

Example: Instrument Cluster Layout Spec

❌ Weak Prompt
Help me design a truck dashboard display.
✅ Strong Prompt
Role: You are a senior automotive HMI designer specializing in commercial vehicle instrument clusters for the Japanese market.

Context: I'm designing the digital instrument cluster for a FUSO Super Great (MY2025 facelift). The display is a 12.3-inch full LCD panel (1920×720). The truck uses the 6R30 12.8L diesel engine with SHIFTPILOT 12-speed AMT. Key systems: Active Brake Assist 6, Active Sideguard Assist 2, Front Blindspot Information System, DPF regeneration, AdBlue (urea SCR). The cluster must comply with ISO 15005 (1.5s max glance time) and ISO 26262 ASIL B for safety-critical telltales.

Task: Propose a visual hierarchy for the cluster layout. Group the information into tiers:
— Tier 1: Always visible (speed, gear, engine RPM, critical warnings)
— Tier 2: Contextual (ADAS status, navigation, DPF regen)
— Tier 3: On-demand (trip computer, settings, maintenance alerts)

For each tier, specify recommended screen zone placement (left / center / right), relative size allocation (% of display), and whether information should be presented as an analog gauge, numeric readout, icon/telltale, or progress bar.

Format: Respond as a structured table, one row per data element.
Constraints: This is for a heavy-duty long-haul truck. Driver cognitive load is the primary concern. Nighttime legibility must be considered.

Example: Generating Telltale State Definitions

✅ CAN Signal → UI State Mapping
Context: Here are the CAN signals for the eCanter battery management system on the instrument cluster bus:

BMS_SOC (0–100%, 1 byte)
BMS_ChargingStatus (0=Not charging, 1=AC, 2=DC)
BMS_TempWarning (0=Normal, 1=High, 2=Critical)
BMS_CellBalancing (0=Inactive, 1=Active)
BMS_RangeEstimate_km (0–300, 2 bytes)

Task: For each signal, define the UI display states for the instrument cluster. Include: display element type (icon, bar, numeric), color coding per state, animation behavior (static, pulsing, flashing), and the ASIL classification if safety-critical. Use the FUSO eCanter's dark-background cluster aesthetic.

Format: One table per signal with columns: Signal Value | Display State | Color | Animation | ASIL

Example: Reviewing a Layout Against Standards

✅ ISO Compliance Check
Role: Automotive HMI standards reviewer.

Context: I've designed a cluster layout where the DPF regeneration status is displayed as a small icon (8×8mm) in the lower-right corner of a 12.3-inch display. The icon changes from grey (inactive) to amber (regen in progress) to red (regen required — pull over).

Task: Review this placement and sizing against ISO 15005 glance-time guidelines and NHTSA recommendations. Specifically: is 8×8mm sufficient for a safety-related telltale at normal driver viewing distance (~700mm)? Should the red state use additional visual cues (animation, size change, audible)? Are there precedents from other Japanese heavy-duty OEMs (Hino Profia, Isuzu Giga) for DPF telltale placement?

Example: Generating UI Copy

✅ Multi-Language Alert Text
Context: FUSO Super Great center display, 7-inch touchscreen. Used for non-critical driver information and settings.

Task: Write the on-screen text for a "Low AdBlue" warning sequence with 3 escalation levels:
1. Advisory (>500km remaining)
2. Warning (100–500km)
3. Critical (<100km, torque derestriction imminent)

For each level, provide: headline text (max 20 characters), body text (max 60 characters), and recommended icon description. Provide in both English and Japanese. Keep text scannable — driver will read in <1.5 seconds at highway speed.
Pattern

Notice every strong prompt follows the same shape: Role → Context (with specific model names, signal names, dimensions) → Task (precise deliverable) → Format (table, JSON, list) → Constraints (standards, cognitive load, screen size). You can reuse this skeleton for any HMI design task.

Chapter 08

Advanced Techniques

Few-Shot Prompting

Include 2–3 examples of the pattern you want before giving the actual task. This is especially useful for consistent formatting across a design system.

✅ Few-Shot for Icon Descriptions
I need icon descriptions for our FUSO HMI design system. Here's the format:

Engine Oil Pressure Warning: Oil can shape, fill indicates level. Red = critical, amber = low. 24×24px minimum. ASIL B.
Parking Brake: Circle with "P" inset, red when engaged, grey when released. 20×20px minimum. ASIL A.

Now create entries in the same format for: Exhaust Brake Active, Differential Lock Engaged, Air Dryer Warning, and PTO (Power Take-Off) Active.

Chain-of-Thought

Ask the model to reason step by step. Useful for complex layout decisions or safety analysis.

✅ Chain-of-Thought for Layout
I need to fit 4 new ADAS telltales into the Super Great cluster. Available space is a 180×40px strip at the top of the display. The telltales are: Active Brake Assist, Sideguard Assist, Lane Departure Warning, and Adaptive Cruise Control.

Think through this step by step:
1. Calculate available space per telltale at 180×40px for 4 items
2. Consider minimum touch/glance target size per ISO 15005
3. Determine if all 4 fit, or if some should be contextual (shown only when active)
4. Propose a layout with spacing, and flag any that should be always-visible vs contextual

Prompt Chaining

Break complex tasks into a sequence. First prompt: "Extract all telltale requirements from this spec document." Second prompt: "Now group them by priority tier." Third prompt: "Now propose screen placement for each group." More reliable than asking everything at once.

Chapter 09

Context Engineering

The LLM is the CPU, and its context window is the RAM. Context engineering is the art of filling the context window with just the right information for the next step.
— Andrej Karpathy

Practical tips: Paste the relevant section of a spec, not the whole 200-page PDF. Summarize long conversations before continuing. Chapter 10

Hallucinations & Verification

LLMs generate statistically likely text — not verified truth. They can confidently cite a nonexistent ISO clause, invent a CAN signal name, or misstate an ASIL requirement. This is called hallucination, and it is an inherent property of how these models work.

For HMI design on safety-rated vehicles, this is not optional to manage — it's critical.

Never trust an LLM's citation of a specific ISO clause number without checking it yourself. The model may correctly describe the intent of a standard while fabricating the exact clause reference. Similarly, if it generates CAN signal names, verify them against your actual DBC file.

Chapter 11

Workflow & Checklist

Before Prompting

1. What exactly do I need? (Layout spec? Icon set? Copy? Standards review?)
2. What context does the model need? (Vehicle model, display size, CAN signals, ISO requirements)
3. What format should the output be? (Table, JSON, prose, Figma-ready spec)
4. Is this safety-critical? (If yes, plan to verify every claim against source documents)

After Getting Output

1. Does this match what I asked for?
2. Are specific standards, signal names, or dimensions accurate? (Check any that matter)
3. Does the reasoning hold up?
4. What's missing that I should add in a follow-up?
5. For safety-critical items: has a qualified engineer independently verified?

Where LLMs Fit in Your HMI Workflow

Concept phase: Brainstorm layout hierarchies, generate telltale state matrices, explore icon metaphors for unfamiliar signals (e.g., hydrogen fuel cell status for the Hino Profia Z FCV).

Specification phase: Draft component specifications from CAN signal lists, generate UI copy across escalation levels, produce design-system-consistent naming conventions.

Review phase: Ask the model to critique your design against glance-time standards, flag potential cognitive overload in your layout, or compare your approach against competitor clusters (Hino, Isuzu Giga, UD Quon).

Documentation: Generate design rationale documents, write handoff specs for developers, produce test case descriptions for HMI validation against ASIL B requirements.

The people who get the most out of these tools tend to be the ones who already think clearly about what they need. The LLM handles the throughput; you handle the judgment.
